Abstract

We propose that gravity is an emergent phenomenon resulting from the universe optimizing for computational efficiency. In this framework, classical spacetime is a compressed representation of high-dimensional quantum information, optimized to avoid the intractable synchronization costs (the "semaphore bottleneck") of large-scale entanglement. To test this "Compression Hypothesis," we developed a high-fidelity, GPU-accelerated computational framework that evaluates candidate gravitational theories against a comprehensive suite of analytical and solver-based validators, using the Kerr metric as the astrophysical baseline.

Our results demonstrate that Asymptotic Safety (AS) uniquely matches the performance of General Relativity across all classical tests (scoring 92.9%), while simultaneously providing a UV-complete quantum framework. This motivates our leading candidate theory: Asymptotically Safe Entropic Compression with Decoherence (ASEC-D). ASEC-D integrates the Renormalization Group flow of AS with entropic corrections modulated by a complexity-driven decoherence factor. This model predicts a novel, testable phenomenon: an entropy-dependent violation of the Weak Equivalence Principle, which can be verified using contemporary atom interferometry.

1. Introduction: The Computational Universe and the Semaphore Bottleneck

The dichotomy between the vast, high-dimensional quantum reality and the smooth, 4D classical spacetime we observe is a central puzzle in modern physics. Principles like the holographic principle suggest that this dimensional reduction is a fundamental feature of nature—an act of supreme information compression.

We posit that gravity is not merely the curvature of spacetime, but the emergent computational process that enforces this compression. Furthermore, we propose a mechanism for this emergence: the universe optimizes for computational efficiency by minimizing the synchronization costs associated with resolving entangled quantum states.

We introduce the analogy of quantum entanglement as "Nature's Semaphore." In concurrent computing, a semaphore manages access to shared resources. If too many threads attempt access, the system bottlenecks. Similarly, as the complexity (entropy) of a multi-agent quantum system increases, the computational cost of synchronizing entangled states—resolving the semaphore—grows exponentially.

To avoid this intractable complexity, the universe enforces decoherence, collapsing the system into a computationally cheaper, compressed classical geometry. This mechanism explains why quantum effects are dominant at microscopic scales (low semaphore contention) but vanish macroscopically, where classical General Relativity (GR) prevails.

The Compression Hypothesis: Gravity emerges as the mechanism compressing high-dimensional quantum states into efficient classical spacetime to avoid computational bottlenecks.

2. The Comprehensive Validation Framework

To test the Compression Hypothesis, we require a rigorous framework capable of evaluating candidate theories against the full spectrum of known physics. Our initial attempts, utilizing concepts like Linear Signal Loss (LSL), while conceptually useful, failed fundamental tests such as energy conservation. This led to the development of a comprehensive, PyTorch-based validation pipeline.

2.1 Methodology Overhaul: From Dual-Baseline to Comprehensive Validation

The framework has evolved significantly from earlier versions that relied on a dual-baseline (Schwarzschild/Reissner-Nordström) and FFT loss metrics. The current methodology employs a robust suite of validators designed to test both classical consistency and quantum predictions.

The Kerr Baseline

We utilize the Kerr metric as the primary baseline, representing the established "gold standard" for astrophysical black holes. Trajectory loss is calculated as the Mean Squared Error (MSE) against Kerr geodesics.

Analytical Validators

Theories must pass rigorous analytical tests, including PPN parameters, Mercury precession, light deflection, gravitational wave consistency, and neutron star observations (PSR J0740).

Solver-Based & Quantum Tests

We employ specialized solvers for high-precision trajectory integration, quantum geodesic simulations (via PennyLane integration), and cosmological tests (CMB power spectrum, primordial GWs).

2.2 Multi-Particle Geodesic Integration

The core computational task is solving the geodesic equation:

d²xμ/dλ² + Γμνρ (dxν/dλ)(dxρ/dλ) = 0

We utilize high-precision (float64) solvers optimized for both symmetric (4D) and general (6D) metrics. Crucially, the framework tests the trajectories of multiple particle types (Protons, Electrons, Neutrinos, and Photons) to ensure the theory respects the universality of free fall and correctly handles massless particles.

3.2 Key Findings

The most significant result is the performance of Asymptotic Safety (AS). It is the only quantum gravity candidate to achieve a score of 92.9%, identical to the classical baselines of Schwarzschild and Kerr. It passes all analytical tests and nearly all solver-based tests, with a trajectory loss against Kerr that is effectively zero (5.18e-45).

This is a crucial finding. Asymptotic Safety provides a mathematically rigorous, QFT-based framework for a UV-complete theory of gravity. Unlike ad-hoc modifications (such as the earlier Linear Signal Loss models), AS inherently conserves energy and respects fundamental symmetries. Its success in the validation pipeline strongly suggests that the Renormalization Group (RG) flow—the mechanism central to AS—is the correct mathematical realization of the "Compression Hypothesis."

Other quantum theories, such as String Theory and Non-Commutative Geometry, also performed well (85.7%), but failed specific solver-based tests (e.g., CMB Power Spectrum), indicating potential inconsistencies in cosmological regimes.

4. ASEC-D: The Leading Candidate Theory

The success of Asymptotic Safety, combined with the conceptual framework of the Compression Hypothesis and the Semaphore Bottleneck, leads us to propose a specific, unified model: Asymptotically Safe Entropic Compression with Decoherence (ASEC-D).

4.1 Mathematical Structure

ASEC-D combines three critical components, formalized in the metric function F(r, S):

1. Asymptotic Safety (RG Flow)

The gravitational constant G runs with scale (G(r)). This represents the scale-dependent compression of spacetime and ensures UV completeness. This is the component validated in Section 3.

2. Entropic Correction (Semaphore Cost)

A logarithmic correction term, derived from QFT entropy calculations, quantifies the base computational cost of quantum synchronization. It introduces a coupling constant β.

3. Decoherence Factor (The Bottleneck)

A factor D(S) = e-(S/Scrit) that suppresses quantum corrections as the internal entropy (S) of a system increases, modeling the computational bottleneck that forces classical emergence.

The combined metric function is:

F(r, S) = FAS(r) + [β (rs/r) ln(r/rs)] ⋅ D(S)

4.2 Behavior and Implications

This model elegantly addresses the feedback from astrophysical observations:

  • Microscopic Systems (Low S): D(S) ≈ 1. The semaphore is efficient. Entropic/quantum corrections to gravity are active.
  • Macroscopic Systems (High S): D(S) → 0. The bottleneck enforces decoherence. Quantum corrections vanish, recovering classical GR (Kerr), explaining why Kerr is "good enough" for astrophysical black holes.
  • Energy Conservation: By building on the Asymptotic Safety framework, the theory remains energetically consistent and avoids the pitfalls of earlier models like LSL.

5. Experimental Verification: The Definitive Test

A unified theory demands an extremely high burden of proof. ASEC-D makes a specific, falsifiable prediction that deviates from General Relativity and is testable with current technology. If gravity is sensitive to the computational cost (entropy) of a system, then gravity must couple to internal information content, not just mass-energy.

5.1 The Prediction: Entropy-Dependent Violation of the WEP

The Weak Equivalence Principle (WEP) states that all objects fall identically, regardless of composition. ASEC-D predicts a violation because the metric F(r, S) explicitly depends on the internal entropy (S) of the falling object.

MGravitational = MInertial + ΔMInfo(S)

5.2 The Experiment: Quantum Atom Interferometry

The definitive test involves measuring the free-fall of two ensembles of the same atomic species (e.g., Rubidium-87) with identical inertial mass but different internal entropy:

Ensemble A: Low Complexity

A Bose-Einstein Condensate (BEC). Highly ordered, near-zero entropy (SA ≈ 0).

Ensemble B: High Complexity

A thermal gas. Highly disordered, significant entropy (SB ≫ 0).

We measure the differential acceleration Δa = aA - aB using high-precision atom interferometry.

The Verdict: General Relativity strictly predicts Δa = 0. The ASEC-D Compression Hypothesis predicts Δa ≠ 0, proportional to the coupling β and the entropy difference.

A confirmed non-zero Δa would provide definitive proof that gravity couples to information complexity, validating the hypothesis that spacetime geometry is an emergent consequence of computational optimization.

6. Conclusion

We have introduced the Compression Hypothesis, framing gravity as an emergent mechanism that optimizes computational cost by compressing quantum information into classical spacetime. This is driven by the need to avoid the "semaphore bottleneck" of large-scale entanglement.

Our comprehensive validation framework rigorously tested this hypothesis, identifying Asymptotic Safety as the leading mathematical realization, matching GR's predictive power while offering quantum completeness. This led to the formulation of ASEC-D, a theory that integrates RG flow, entropic corrections, and complexity-driven decoherence.

ASEC-D provides a coherent explanation for the quantum-to-classical transition and offers a definitive, near-term experimental test via atom interferometry, paving a concrete path toward a provable unified theory of gravity.
